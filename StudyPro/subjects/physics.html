Grade 7 Physics Notes

Forces and Motion

Introduction to Forces

A force is a push or pull that can cause an object to move, stop, or change direction. Forces are measured in newtons (N).

Newton's First Law: An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and direction unless acted upon by an unbalanced force.

Types of Forces

  • Gravity: The force that attracts objects toward each other. On Earth, gravity pulls objects downward.
  • Friction: The force that opposes motion between surfaces that are in contact.
  • Normal force: The force exerted by a surface on an object in contact with it.
  • Applied force: A force applied to an object by a person or another object.

Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

When forces on an object are balanced (equal in opposite directions), the object will not change its motion. When forces are unbalanced, the object will accelerate in the direction of the net force.

Example:

A book is sitting on a table. The force of gravity pulls it downward with 10 N. What is the normal force exerted by the table on the book?

Since the book is not moving, the forces must be balanced. The normal force is 10 N upward, balancing the 10 N of gravity.

Newton's Second Law

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.

F = m × a

where:

F = net force (N)

m = mass (kg)

a = acceleration (m/s²)

Example:

A 5 kg object experiences a net force of 15 N. What is its acceleration?

a = F/m = 15 N / 5 kg = 3 m/s²

Newton's Third Law

For every action (force), there is an equal and opposite reaction (force).

Example:

When you push on a wall, the wall pushes back on you with equal force. This is why you don't move through the wall when you push on it.

What is Energy?

Energy is the ability to do work or cause change. It exists in many forms and can be transformed from one form to another. Energy is measured in joules (J).

Types of Energy

  • Kinetic Energy: Energy of motion. Examples include running, wind, and moving vehicles.
  • Potential Energy: Stored energy due to position or condition. Examples include a book on a shelf or a stretched rubber band.
  • Thermal Energy: Energy of moving particles in matter, related to temperature.
  • Chemical Energy: Energy stored in chemical bonds. Examples include food, batteries, and fuel.
  • Electrical Energy: Energy from electric charges. Examples include lightning and electrical appliances.
  • Sound Energy: Energy from vibrations traveling through matter.
  • Light Energy: Energy from electromagnetic waves. Examples include sunlight and light bulbs.
  • Nuclear Energy: Energy stored in atomic nuclei.

Kinetic Energy: KE = (1/2) × m × v²

where:

m = mass (kg)

v = velocity (m/s)

Gravitational Potential Energy: PE = m × g × h

where:

m = mass (kg)

g = gravitational acceleration (9.8 m/s²)

h = height (m)

Energy Transformations

Energy can change from one form to another. Here are some examples:

  • A ball rolling down a hill: Gravitational potential energy → Kinetic energy
  • Burning wood: Chemical energy → Thermal energy + Light energy
  • Solar panels: Light energy → Electrical energy
  • A battery in a flashlight: Chemical energy → Electrical energy → Light energy

Example:

Calculate the kinetic energy of a 60 kg person running at 3 m/s.

KE = (1/2) × m × v²

KE = (1/2) × 60 kg × (3 m/s)²

KE = (1/2) × 60 kg × 9 m²/s²

KE = 30 kg × 9 m²/s²

KE = 270 J

Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another. The total amount of energy in an isolated system remains constant.

Example:

A 2 kg ball is held 10 m above the ground. Calculate its potential energy and the kinetic energy just before it hits the ground (ignoring air resistance).

Initial Potential Energy: PE = m × g × h = 2 kg × 9.8 m/s² × 10 m = 196 J

By conservation of energy, if all PE is converted to KE:

Final Kinetic Energy = Initial Potential Energy = 196 J

Practice Problems

  1. What is the kinetic energy of a 1,500 kg car traveling at 25 m/s?
  2. How high must a 0.5 kg object be lifted to have a potential energy of 24.5 J?
  3. A pendulum has a mass of 0.2 kg and swings to a maximum height of 0.3 m. What is its maximum kinetic energy at the lowest point of its swing?

Solutions:

1. KE = (1/2) × m × v² = (1/2) × 1,500 kg × (25 m/s)² = 468,750 J

2. PE = m × g × h, so h = PE / (m × g) = 24.5 J / (0.5 kg × 9.8 m/s²) = 5 m

3. At maximum height, PE = m × g × h = 0.2 kg × 9.8 m/s² × 0.3 m = 0.588 J. By conservation of energy, the maximum KE at the lowest point equals the PE at the highest point = 0.588 J.

Grade 8 Physics Notes

Basic Electricity

Introduction to Electricity

Electricity is a form of energy resulting from the existence of charged particles. There are two types of electric charge: positive and negative.

Electric Charge

  • Like charges repel each other (positive-positive or negative-negative)
  • Unlike charges attract each other (positive-negative)
  • The unit of electric charge is the coulomb (C)

Electric Current

Electric current is the flow of electric charge through a conductor. It's measured in amperes (A).

I = Q / t

where:

I = current (A)

Q = charge (C)

t = time (s)

Voltage

Voltage (electric potential difference) is the "pressure" that pushes electric charges through a circuit. It's measured in volts (V).

Resistance

Resistance is a measure of how much a material opposes the flow of electric current. It's measured in ohms (Ω).

Ohm's Law: V = I × R

where:

V = voltage (V)

I = current (A)

R = resistance (Ω)

Example:

A circuit has a resistance of 5 Ω and a current of 2 A. What is the voltage?

V = I × R = 2 A × 5 Ω = 10 V

Electric Circuits

An electric circuit is a complete path through which electric current can flow. The main components of a circuit include:

  • Power Source: Provides energy (e.g., battery, generator)
  • Conductors: Materials that allow electric current to flow easily (e.g., copper wires)
  • Load: Device that converts electrical energy to another form (e.g., light bulb, motor)
  • Switch: Controls the flow of current by opening or closing the circuit

Series and Parallel Circuits

Series Circuit: Components are connected one after another in a single path.

  • The same current flows through all components
  • Total voltage equals the sum of individual voltage drops
  • Total resistance equals the sum of individual resistances

For resistors in series: Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...

Parallel Circuit: Components are connected across the same voltage source with multiple paths for current.

  • The voltage is the same across all components
  • Total current equals the sum of individual currents
  • Total resistance is less than the smallest individual resistance

For resistors in parallel: 1/Rtotal = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...

Example:

Calculate the total resistance for three resistors connected in series: 3 Ω, 5 Ω, and 7 Ω.

Rtotal = 3 Ω + 5 Ω + 7 Ω = 15 Ω

Now calculate the total resistance if the same resistors are connected in parallel:

1/Rtotal = 1/3 Ω + 1/5 Ω + 1/7 Ω = 0.333 + 0.2 + 0.143 = 0.676

Rtotal = 1/0.676 = 1.48 Ω

Grade 9 Physics Notes

Waves and Sound

Types of Waves

A wave is a disturbance that transfers energy from one place to another without transferring matter. There are two main types of waves:

  • Mechanical Waves: Require a medium to travel through (e.g., sound waves, water waves)
  • Electromagnetic Waves: Don't require a medium (e.g., light, radio waves, X-rays)

Waves can also be categorized by how they move:

  • Transverse Waves: Particles of the medium move perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation (e.g., light waves)
  • Longitudinal Waves: Particles of the medium move parallel to the direction of wave propagation (e.g., sound waves)

Wave Properties

  • Wavelength (λ): Distance between two consecutive points that are in phase (e.g., crest to crest)
  • Frequency (f): Number of complete waves passing a point per second, measured in hertz (Hz)
  • Amplitude: Maximum displacement from equilibrium
  • Period (T): Time taken for one complete wave to pass a point
  • Wave speed (v): Speed at which the wave travels

v = λ × f

T = 1/f

Example:

A wave has a frequency of 20 Hz and a wavelength of 0.5 m. Calculate its speed.

v = λ × f = 0.5 m × 20 Hz = 10 m/s

Sound Waves

Sound is a longitudinal wave that travels through a medium by compressing and expanding it. Sound cannot travel through a vacuum.

  • Speed of sound: Approximately 343 m/s in air at 20°C
  • Audible range: Humans can typically hear frequencies between 20 Hz and 20,000 Hz
  • Infrasound: Sound waves with frequencies below 20 Hz
  • Ultrasound: Sound waves with frequencies above 20,000 Hz

Sound Properties

  • Pitch: Determined by frequency - higher frequency means higher pitch
  • Volume/Loudness: Determined by amplitude - greater amplitude means louder sound
  • Timbre/Quality: Determined by the waveform - allows us to distinguish between different instruments playing the same note

Wave Phenomena

  • Reflection: Waves bounce off a surface (e.g., echoes)
  • Refraction: Waves change direction when passing from one medium to another
  • Diffraction: Waves bend around obstacles or through openings
  • Interference: When two waves meet, they can reinforce (constructive) or cancel (destructive) each other
  • Resonance: Increased amplitude when an object is forced to vibrate at its natural frequency

Example:

Calculate the wavelength of a sound wave with a frequency of 440 Hz (concert A) in air where the speed of sound is 343 m/s.

λ = v/f = 343 m/s ÷ 440 Hz = 0.78 m

Work, Energy, and Power

Work is done when a force moves an object over a distance. Energy is the ability to do work, and power is the rate at which work is done.

Work: W = F × d × cos θ

where:

W = work (J)

F = force (N)

d = displacement (m)

θ = angle between force and displacement

Power: P = W/t = F × v

where:

P = power (W)

t = time (s)

v = velocity (m/s)

Momentum and Impulse

Momentum is the product of mass and velocity. Impulse is the change in momentum.

Momentum: p = m × v

Impulse: J = F × t = Δp = m × Δv

Conservation of Momentum: m₁v₁i + m₂v₂i = m₁v₁f + m₂v₂f

Example:

A 60 kg person catches a 0.5 kg ball moving at 30 m/s. If the ball comes to rest, what velocity does the person acquire?

By conservation of momentum:

mballvball + mpersonvperson,i = mballvball,f + mpersonvperson,f

0.5 kg × 30 m/s + 60 kg × 0 m/s = 0.5 kg × 0 m/s + 60 kg × vperson,f

15 kg·m/s = 60 kg × vperson,f

vperson,f = 15 kg·m/s ÷ 60 kg = 0.25 m/s

Circular Motion

An object moving in a circle experiences centripetal acceleration toward the center of the circle.

Centripetal Acceleration: ac = v²/r = ω²r

Centripetal Force: Fc = mac = mv²/r

Angular Velocity: ω = v/r = 2πf

Period: T = 2πr/v = 1/f

where:

ω = angular velocity (rad/s)

f = frequency (Hz)

T = period (s)

Example:

A 1,500 kg car travels around a circular track with a radius of 50 m at a speed of 20 m/s. Calculate the centripetal force required.

Fc = mv²/r = 1,500 kg × (20 m/s)² ÷ 50 m = 1,500 kg × 400 m²/s² ÷ 50 m = 12,000 N

Simple Harmonic Motion

Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is a type of periodic motion where the restoring force is proportional to the displacement and in the opposite direction.

F = -kx

ω² = k/m

T = 2π√(m/k)

f = 1/T = (1/2π)√(k/m)

where:

k = spring constant (N/m)

x = displacement (m)

Examples of SHM include:

  • A mass on a spring
  • A pendulum (for small angles)
  • Vibrating strings

Example:

A mass of 0.2 kg is attached to a spring with a spring constant of 80 N/m. Calculate the period of oscillation.

T = 2π√(m/k) = 2π√(0.2 kg / 80 N/m) = 2π√(0.0025 s²) = 2π × 0.05 s = 0.314 s

Practice Problems

  1. A 70 kg person climbs a flight of stairs 5 m high in 8 seconds. Calculate the power output.
  2. A 1,200 kg car moving at 25 m/s collides with a stationary 800 kg car. If they stick together after collision, what is their common velocity?
  3. A 0.5 kg mass is attached to a spring and oscillates with a period of 0.4 s. Calculate the spring constant.

Solutions:

1. W = mgh = 70 kg × 9.8 m/s² × 5 m = 3,430 J. P = W/t = 3,430 J ÷ 8 s = 428.75 W

2. By conservation of momentum: m₁v₁i + m₂v₂i = (m₁ + m₂)vf. 1,200 kg × 25 m/s + 800 kg × 0 m/s = (1,200 kg + 800 kg)vf. 30,000 kg·m/s = 2,000 kg × vf. vf = 15 m/s

3. T = 2π√(m/k), so k = 4π²m/T². k = 4π² × 0.5 kg ÷ (0.4 s)² = 123.4 N/m

Grade 10 Physics Notes

Energy Transformations

Energy Transformation and Conservation

Energy transformation is the process of changing energy from one form to another. The law of conservation of energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another.

Common Energy Transformations

  • Electrical to Thermal: Electric heater, toaster
  • Electrical to Mechanical: Electric motor
  • Electrical to Light: Light bulb, LED
  • Chemical to Thermal: Burning fuel
  • Chemical to Electrical: Battery
  • Mechanical to Electrical: Generator, dynamo
  • Light to Electrical: Solar panel
  • Gravitational Potential to Kinetic: Falling object
  • Kinetic to Thermal: Friction

Efficiency

Efficiency is a measure of how much of the input energy is converted to useful output energy. It's usually expressed as a percentage.

Efficiency = (Useful output energy / Total input energy) × 100%

Example:

A light bulb converts 5 J of electrical energy into 1 J of light energy and 4 J of thermal energy. Calculate its efficiency as a light source.

Efficiency = (Useful output energy / Total input energy) × 100%

Efficiency = (1 J / 5 J) × 100% = 20%

Energy Resources

Energy resources can be classified as non-renewable or renewable:

Non-renewable Resources:

  • Fossil Fuels (coal, oil, natural gas): Formed from remains of plants and animals over millions of years
  • Nuclear Fuel: Uranium used in nuclear power plants

Renewable Resources:

  • Solar Energy: Energy from the sun
  • Wind Energy: Kinetic energy of moving air
  • Hydroelectric Energy: Energy from flowing water
  • Geothermal Energy: Heat energy from within the Earth
  • Biomass: Energy from organic materials
  • Tidal Energy: Energy from ocean tides

Environmental Impact

Different energy sources have varying environmental impacts:

  • Fossil Fuels: Produce carbon dioxide when burned, contributing to climate change
  • Nuclear Energy: Produces radioactive waste that requires safe storage
  • Renewable Energy: Generally has lower environmental impact but may have issues like land use (solar farms), noise (wind turbines), or habitat disruption (hydroelectric dams)

Example:

A hydroelectric power plant converts 80% of the gravitational potential energy of falling water into electrical energy. If water falls from a height of 50 m at a rate of 1,000 kg/s, calculate the electrical power output.

Input power = Rate of work done by gravity = mgh per second = 1,000 kg/s × 9.8 m/s² × 50 m = 490,000 W

Electrical power output = 80% of input power = 0.8 × 490,000 W = 392,000 W = 392 kW

Grade 11 Physics Notes

Nuclear Physics

Atomic Structure

Atoms consist of a nucleus (containing protons and neutrons) surrounded by electrons. The nucleus accounts for nearly all of the atom's mass but only a tiny fraction of its volume.

  • Protons: Positively charged particles, mass ≈ 1.673 × 10-27 kg
  • Neutrons: Neutral particles, mass ≈ 1.675 × 10-27 kg
  • Electrons: Negatively charged particles, mass ≈ 9.109 × 10-31 kg (about 1/1836 of a proton)

Atomic Number (Z): Number of protons in the nucleus

Mass Number (A): Number of protons + neutrons in the nucleus

Isotopes: Atoms with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons

Nuclear Forces

The nucleus is held together by the strong nuclear force, which overcomes the electrostatic repulsion between positively charged protons at short distances.

Radioactivity

Radioactivity is the spontaneous emission of radiation from unstable nuclei. The three main types of radiation are:

  • Alpha (α) radiation: Helium nuclei (2 protons, 2 neutrons), highly ionizing but not very penetrating
  • Beta (β) radiation: Electrons, moderately ionizing and penetrating
  • Gamma (γ) radiation: High-energy electromagnetic waves, weakly ionizing but highly penetrating

Alpha decay: AZX → A-4Z-2Y + 42He

Beta decay (β-): AZX → AZ+1Y + 0-1e + ῡ

Gamma decay: AZX* → AZX + γ

Half-life

The half-life of a radioactive isotope is the time taken for half of the unstable nuclei in a sample to decay.

N = N₀ × (1/2)t/T₁/₂

where:

N = number of undecayed nuclei remaining

N₀ = initial number of undecayed nuclei

t = time elapsed

T₁/₂ = half-life

Example:

A sample initially contains 4.0 × 1020 atoms of a radioactive isotope with a half-life of 5 days. How many atoms remain after 20 days?

N = N₀ × (1/2)t/T₁/₂ = 4.0 × 1020 × (1/2)20/5 = 4.0 × 1020 × (1/2)4 = 4.0 × 1020 × (1/16) = 2.5 × 1019 atoms

Nuclear Reactions

Nuclear Fission: The splitting of a heavy nucleus into lighter nuclei with the release of energy. Used in nuclear power plants.

Example of fission: 23592U + 10n → 14156Ba + 9236Kr + 310n + energy

Nuclear Fusion: The combining of light nuclei to form a heavier nucleus with the release of energy. The process that powers the sun.

Example of fusion: 21H + 31H → 42He + 10n + energy

Mass-Energy Equivalence

Einstein's famous equation relates mass and energy:

E = mc²

where:

E = energy (J)

m = mass (kg)

c = speed of light (3 × 108 m/s)

Example:

Calculate the energy released when 0.0005 kg of mass is converted to energy in a nuclear reaction.

E = mc² = 0.0005 kg × (3 × 108 m/s)² = 0.0005 kg × 9 × 1016 m²/s² = 4.5 × 1013 J

This is equivalent to about 10,000 tons of TNT!

Introduction to Relativity

Premium

Special Relativity

Einstein's theory of special relativity, published in 1905, is based on two postulates:

  1. The laws of physics are the same in all inertial reference frames (frames moving at constant velocity relative to each other).
  2. The speed of light in vacuum is the same for all observers, regardless of their motion or the motion of the light source.

Time Dilation

Moving clocks run slower compared to stationary clocks. This effect becomes significant only at speeds approaching the speed of light.

Δt = Δt₀/√(1 - v²/c²)

where:

Δt = time interval measured by a stationary observer

Δt₀ = proper time interval (time measured in the frame where events occur at the same location)

v = relative velocity between the frames

c = speed of light (3 × 108 m/s)

Example:

A spacecraft travels at 0.8c relative to Earth. If a clock on the spacecraft measures 1 hour, how much time passes on Earth?

Δt = Δt₀/√(1 - v²/c²) = 1 hour/√(1 - (0.8c)²/c²) = 1 hour/√(1 - 0.64) = 1 hour/√0.36 = 1 hour/0.6 = 1.67 hours

Length Contraction

Moving objects appear shortened in the direction of motion.

L = L₀ × √(1 - v²/c²)

where:

L = length measured by a stationary observer

L₀ = proper length (length measured in the object's rest frame)

Example:

A 100-meter-long spacecraft travels at 0.6c relative to Earth. What is its length as measured by an observer on Earth?

L = L₀ × √(1 - v²/c²) = 100 m × √(1 - (0.6c)²/c²) = 100 m × √(1 - 0.36) = 100 m × √0.64 = 100 m × 0.8 = 80 m

Relativistic Mass and Energy

As an object's speed increases, its mass effectively increases from the perspective of a stationary observer.

m = m₀/√(1 - v²/c²)

where:

m = relativistic mass

m₀ = rest mass

The famous mass-energy equivalence formula:

E = mc²

Total energy: E = mc² = m₀c²/√(1 - v²/c²)

Rest energy: E₀ = m₀c²

Kinetic energy: K = E - E₀ = m₀c²(1/√(1 - v²/c²) - 1)

General Relativity

Einstein's theory of general relativity, published in 1915, describes gravity as a curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy.

Key concepts include:

  • Spacetime is a four-dimensional continuum (three space dimensions plus time)
  • Mass and energy curve spacetime
  • Objects move along geodesics (shortest paths) in curved spacetime
  • Gravity is not a force but a manifestation of spacetime curvature

Experimental Confirmations

Several observations have confirmed Einstein's theories:

  • Bending of light around the Sun during solar eclipses
  • Gravitational redshift (light loses energy climbing out of a gravitational field)
  • Precession of Mercury's orbit
  • Gravitational waves (detected by LIGO in 2015)
  • Time dilation in GPS satellites (requires relativistic corrections)

The Twin Paradox

The twin paradox is a thought experiment in special relativity:

  • One twin stays on Earth while the other travels on a high-speed journey through space
  • Due to time dilation, the traveling twin ages less than the Earth-bound twin
  • This is not actually a paradox because the traveling twin experiences acceleration (changing reference frames), breaking the symmetry

Example:

A twin travels to a star 4 light-years away at 0.8c, then returns at the same speed. How much younger will the traveling twin be compared to the Earth-bound twin when they reunite?

Time in Earth frame: 2 × (4 light-years / 0.8c) = 10 years

Time in traveler's frame (each way): Δt₀ = Δt × √(1 - v²/c²) = 5 years × √(1 - 0.64) = 5 years × 0.6 = 3 years

Total traveler time: 2 × 3 = 6 years

Age difference: 10 - 6 = 4 years

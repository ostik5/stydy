Grade 7 Math Notes

Algebra Basics

Variables and Expressions

Variables are symbols (usually letters) that represent unspecified numbers. Algebraic expressions combine variables and numbers using operations like addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division.

Expression: 3x + 5y - 2

Terms: 3x, 5y, -2

Coefficients: 3, 5, -2

Variables: x, y

Evaluating Expressions

To evaluate an expression, substitute the values of the variables and perform the operations.

Example:

Evaluate 3x + 5y - 2 when x = 2 and y = 4

Substitute: 3(2) + 5(4) - 2 = 6 + 20 - 2 = 24

Simplifying Expressions

To simplify an expression, combine like terms (terms with the same variables raised to the same powers).

Example:

Simplify: 3x + 5x - 2x + 7

Combine like terms: 3x + 5x - 2x + 7 = 6x + 7

Key Concepts to Remember

  • Like terms have the same variables with the same exponents
  • The distributive property: a(b + c) = ab + ac
  • When adding/subtracting polynomials, combine like terms
  • When multiplying polynomials, multiply each term in the first polynomial by each term in the second

Introduction to Geometry

Basic Geometric Shapes

Geometry is the study of shapes, sizes, properties, and measurements of figures and spaces. Here are the fundamental shapes:

  • Point: Position in space, no dimensions
  • Line: Straight path extending infinitely in both directions
  • Line segment: Part of a line with two endpoints
  • Ray: Part of a line with one endpoint, extending infinitely in one direction
  • Angle: Formed by two rays with a common endpoint (vertex)

Angles

Angles are measured in degrees (°) or radians.

  • Acute angle: Less than 90°
  • Right angle: Exactly 90°
  • Obtuse angle: Greater than 90° but less than 180°
  • Straight angle: Exactly 180°
  • Reflex angle: Greater than 180° but less than 360°

Sum of angles in a triangle = 180°

Sum of angles in a quadrilateral = 360°

Triangles

A triangle is a polygon with three sides and three angles.

  • Equilateral triangle: All sides and angles are equal (60° each)
  • Isosceles triangle: Two sides and two angles are equal
  • Scalene triangle: All sides and angles are different
  • Right triangle: Contains one right angle (90°)

Area of a triangle = (1/2) × base × height

Perimeter of a triangle = sum of all sides

Example:

Find the area of a triangle with base 6 cm and height 8 cm.

Area = (1/2) × 6 cm × 8 cm = 24 cm²

Basic Statistics

Data Collection and Representation

Statistics is the study of collecting, analyzing, interpreting, and presenting data. Data can be represented in various forms:

  • Tables: Organized arrangement of data in rows and columns
  • Bar graphs: Rectangular bars proportional to the values they represent
  • Line graphs: Points connected by straight lines, showing trends over time
  • Pie charts: Circular chart divided into sectors, each representing a proportion
  • Histograms: Bar graphs showing frequency distributions

Measures of Central Tendency

Measures that describe the center or middle of a data set:

Mean: Sum of all values divided by the number of values

Mean = (x₁ + x₂ + ... + xₙ) / n

Median: Middle value when data is arranged in order

Mode: Value that appears most frequently

Example:

Find the mean, median, and mode of: 5, 8, 3, 8, 4

Mean = (5 + 8 + 3 + 8 + 4) / 5 = 28 / 5 = 5.6

Arranged in order: 3, 4, 5, 8, 8

Median = 5

Mode = 8 (appears twice)

Range and Spread

The range is a measure of the spread or dispersion of a data set.

Range = Maximum value - Minimum value

Example:

Find the range of: 5, 8, 3, 8, 4

Maximum = 8, Minimum = 3

Range = 8 - 3 = 5

Advanced Fractions

Complex Fraction Operations

Advanced fraction operations involve combining multiple operations and working with complex fractions (fractions within fractions).

To simplify a complex fraction: Convert both numerator and denominator to a single fraction, then divide the numerator by the denominator.

Example:

Simplify: (2/3 + 1/4) ÷ (5/6 - 1/3)

Step 1: Simplify the numerator: 2/3 + 1/4 = 8/12 + 3/12 = 11/12

Step 2: Simplify the denominator: 5/6 - 1/3 = 5/6 - 2/6 = 3/6 = 1/2

Step 3: Divide: (11/12) ÷ (1/2) = 11/12 × 2/1 = 22/12 = 11/6

Applications of Fractions

Fractions are used in various real-world situations:

  • Cooking and recipes (e.g., 3/4 cup of flour)
  • Time measurements (e.g., 1/4 of an hour = 15 minutes)
  • Probability (e.g., the probability of getting heads is 1/2)
  • Surveys and statistics (e.g., 2/5 of respondents agreed)

Converting Between Fractions, Decimals, and Percentages

Being able to convert between these formats is essential for many math problems.

Fraction to decimal: Divide numerator by denominator

Decimal to fraction: Write as numerator over power of 10, then simplify

Fraction to percentage: Convert to decimal, then multiply by 100

Percentage to fraction: Divide by 100, then simplify

Examples:

3/4 as a decimal: 3 ÷ 4 = 0.75

0.125 as a fraction: 0.125 = 125/1000 = 1/8

2/5 as a percentage: 2/5 = 0.4 = 40%

65% as a fraction: 65% = 65/100 = 13/20

Practice Problems

Try these advanced fraction problems:

  1. Simplify: (1/2 + 1/3) ÷ (1/4 - 1/8)
  2. Convert 7/11 to a decimal and percentage
  3. If 3/8 of students in a class of 40 are boys, how many girls are there?
  4. Simplify: (2/3)² × (3/4)³ ÷ (1/2)

Solutions:

1. (1/2 + 1/3) ÷ (1/4 - 1/8) = (3/6 + 2/6) ÷ (2/8 - 1/8) = 5/6 ÷ 1/8 = 5/6 × 8/1 = 40/6 = 6⅔

2. 7/11 = 0.636... = 63.64%

3. Boys = 3/8 × 40 = 15, Girls = 40 - 15 = 25

4. (2/3)² × (3/4)³ ÷ (1/2) = 4/9 × 27/64 × 2/1 = 216/576 = 3/8

Grade 8 Math Notes

Linear Equations

Solving One-Step Equations

A one-step equation can be solved using a single operation (addition, subtraction, multiplication, or division).

To solve: x + a = b, subtract a from both sides: x = b - a

To solve: x - a = b, add a to both sides: x = b + a

To solve: ax = b, divide both sides by a: x = b/a

To solve: x/a = b, multiply both sides by a: x = ab

Examples:

x + 5 = 12 → x = 12 - 5 = 7

x - 3 = 9 → x = 9 + 3 = 12

3x = 15 → x = 15 ÷ 3 = 5

x/4 = 6 → x = 6 × 4 = 24

Solving Multi-Step Equations

Multi-step equations require several operations to isolate the variable.

General steps for solving:

  1. Simplify both sides (combine like terms, distribute)
  2. Move all variable terms to one side
  3. Move all constant terms to the other side
  4. Combine like terms
  5. Isolate the variable by dividing both sides by its coefficient

Example:

Solve: 3(x - 2) + 4 = 2x + 7

1. Distribute: 3x - 6 + 4 = 2x + 7

2. Combine like terms: 3x - 2 = 2x + 7

3. Subtract 2x from both sides: 3x - 2x - 2 = 2x - 2x + 7

4. Simplify: x - 2 = 7

5. Add 2 to both sides: x = 9

Word Problems with Linear Equations

Steps for solving word problems:

  1. Define the variable (what are you trying to find?)
  2. Write an equation representing the problem
  3. Solve the equation
  4. Check your answer in the context of the problem

Example:

A number plus 15 is equal to twice the number minus 3. Find the number.

1. Let x = the number

2. Write the equation: x + 15 = 2x - 3

3. Solve for x: x + 15 = 2x - 3

Subtract x from both sides: 15 = x - 3

Add 3 to both sides: 18 = x

4. Check: 18 + 15 = 33 and 2(18) - 3 = 36 - 3 = 33 ✓

Grade 9 Math Notes

Quadratic Equations

Standard Form and Key Features

A quadratic equation has the standard form ax² + bx + c = 0, where a, b, and c are constants and a ≠ 0.

Quadratic Function: f(x) = ax² + bx + c

Vertex Form: f(x) = a(x - h)² + k, where (h, k) is the vertex

Factored Form: f(x) = a(x - r₁)(x - r₂), where r₁ and r₂ are the roots

Key features of a quadratic function:

  • Vertex: The highest or lowest point on the parabola, occurs at x = -b/(2a)
  • Axis of symmetry: The vertical line passing through the vertex, x = -b/(2a)
  • y-intercept: The point where the parabola crosses the y-axis, (0, c)
  • x-intercepts/roots: The points where the parabola crosses the x-axis, found by solving ax² + bx + c = 0

Solving Quadratic Equations

There are several methods to solve quadratic equations:

  1. Factoring: If ax² + bx + c can be factored as a(x - r₁)(x - r₂) = 0, then x = r₁ or x = r₂
  2. Square Root Method: If ax² = b, then x = ±√(b/a)
  3. Completing the Square: Rewrite the equation in the form a(x + p)² = q, then solve
  4. Quadratic Formula: x = (-b ± √(b² - 4ac)) / (2a)

Quadratic Formula: x = (-b ± √(b² - 4ac)) / (2a)

Discriminant: b² - 4ac determines the number and type of solutions:

- If b² - 4ac > 0: Two distinct real roots

- If b² - 4ac = 0: One repeated real root

- If b² - 4ac < 0: Two complex conjugate roots

Example:

Solve: 2x² - 7x + 3 = 0

Using the quadratic formula with a = 2, b = -7, c = 3:

x = (-(-7) ± √((-7)² - 4(2)(3))) / (2(2))

x = (7 ± √(49 - 24)) / 4

x = (7 ± √25) / 4

x = (7 ± 5) / 4

x = 3 or x = 1/2

Applications of Quadratic Equations

Quadratic equations can model various real-world situations:

  • Projectile motion (height of an object over time)
  • Area and perimeter problems
  • Revenue and profit functions
  • Speed, time, and distance relationships

Example:

A ball is thrown upward from a height of 6 meters with an initial velocity of 15 m/s. The height h (in meters) after t seconds is given by h = -4.9t² + 15t + 6. When will the ball hit the ground?

When the ball hits the ground, h = 0:

0 = -4.9t² + 15t + 6

4.9t² - 15t - 6 = 0

Using the quadratic formula:

t = (15 ± √(15² - 4(4.9)(-6))) / (2(4.9))

t = (15 ± √(225 + 117.6)) / 9.8

t = (15 ± √342.6) / 9.8

t = (15 ± 18.51) / 9.8

t = 3.42 seconds or t = -0.36 seconds

Since we can't have negative time, the ball hits the ground after approximately 3.42 seconds.

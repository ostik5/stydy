Grade 7 Chemistry Notes

States of Matter

Introduction to Matter

Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space. It is made up of tiny particles called atoms and molecules. Matter exists in three main states: solid, liquid, and gas.

Properties of the Three States of Matter

Property Solid Liquid Gas
Shape Definite Takes the shape of container Takes the shape of container
Volume Definite Definite Fills the entire container
Particle arrangement Tightly packed in fixed positions Close together but can move around Far apart, moving freely
Particle movement Vibrate in place Slide past each other Move rapidly in all directions
Density Highest Medium Lowest
Compressibility Very slight Slight High

Changes of State

Matter can change from one state to another when energy is added or removed.

  • Melting: Solid → Liquid (energy added)
  • Freezing: Liquid → Solid (energy removed)
  • Vaporization/Evaporation: Liquid → Gas (energy added)
  • Condensation: Gas → Liquid (energy removed)
  • Sublimation: Solid → Gas (energy added)
  • Deposition: Gas → Solid (energy removed)

Example:

What happens to the particles of water when it changes from a liquid to a gas?

When water changes from a liquid to a gas (vaporization), the water particles gain energy. This causes them to move faster and overcome the attractive forces between them. The particles then spread out and move freely, becoming water vapor.

Physical vs. Chemical Changes

Physical changes alter the form of a substance but not its chemical composition. Examples include changes in state, dissolving, and physical deformation.

Chemical changes involve the formation of new substances with different properties. Signs of chemical changes include color change, gas production, energy change (heat/light), and precipitate formation.

Example:

Classify the following as physical or chemical changes:

  1. Melting ice cream - Physical change (state change)
  2. Rusting of iron - Chemical change (new substance formed)
  3. Dissolving sugar in water - Physical change (sugar can be recovered by evaporation)
  4. Burning of paper - Chemical change (creates ash, smoke, and heat)

Grade 8 Chemistry Notes

Elements and Compounds

Elements

An element is a pure substance that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means. Each element consists of only one type of atom and has unique properties.

Elements are organized in the periodic table based on their properties and atomic structure.

The Periodic Table

The periodic table arranges elements according to their atomic number (number of protons) and groups them based on similar properties.

  • Periods (rows): Elements in the same period have the same number of electron shells.
  • Groups (columns): Elements in the same group have similar chemical properties and the same number of electrons in their outer shell.

Atoms and Their Structure

Atoms are the basic building blocks of matter. They consist of:

  • Protons: Positively charged particles in the nucleus
  • Neutrons: Neutral particles in the nucleus
  • Electrons: Negatively charged particles orbiting the nucleus in energy levels or shells

Atomic Number = Number of Protons

Mass Number = Number of Protons + Number of Neutrons

Number of Electrons = Number of Protons (in a neutral atom)

Example:

The element oxygen has 8 protons and 8 neutrons. What is its atomic number and mass number?

Atomic Number = 8 (number of protons)

Mass Number = 8 + 8 = 16 (protons + neutrons)

Compounds

A compound is a substance formed when two or more elements are chemically combined in a fixed ratio. The properties of compounds are different from those of their constituent elements.

Chemical Formulas

Chemical formulas show the elements present in a compound and their relative numbers.

Examples:

H2O (water): 2 hydrogen atoms and 1 oxygen atom

CO2 (carbon dioxide): 1 carbon atom and 2 oxygen atoms

NaCl (table salt): 1 sodium atom and 1 chlorine atom

Mixtures vs. Pure Substances

A pure substance has a fixed composition and properties. It can be an element or a compound.

A mixture contains two or more substances that are physically combined but not chemically bonded. The components can be separated by physical means.

  • Homogeneous mixtures (solutions) have uniform composition throughout.
  • Heterogeneous mixtures have visibly different parts with different compositions.

Examples:

Pure substances: Gold (element), Water (compound)

Homogeneous mixtures: Salt water, Air

Heterogeneous mixtures: Soil, Oil and water, Salad

Grade 9 Chemistry Notes

Chemical Reactions

Introduction to Chemical Reactions

A chemical reaction is a process where substances (reactants) are transformed into different substances (products) through the breaking and forming of chemical bonds.

Reactants → Products

Signs of Chemical Reactions

  • Color change
  • Production of a gas (bubbling)
  • Formation of a precipitate (solid)
  • Temperature change (heat absorbed or released)
  • Light emission

Balancing Chemical Equations

In a chemical equation, the number of atoms of each element must be the same on both sides (conservation of mass).

Steps to balance an equation:

  1. Count the number of atoms of each element on both sides
  2. Add coefficients (numbers in front of formulas) to balance the atoms
  3. Start with the most complex formula
  4. Check that all atoms are balanced

Example:

Balance the equation: H2 + O2 → H2O

Count atoms: Left side has 2 H and 2 O. Right side has 2 H and 1 O.

Oxygen is not balanced. Add coefficient 2 to H2O:

H2 + O2 → 2H2O

Now count again: Left side has 2 H and 2 O. Right side has 4 H and 2 O.

Hydrogen is not balanced. Add coefficient 2 to H2:

2H2 + O2 → 2H2O

Final count: Left side has 4 H and 2 O. Right side has 4 H and 2 O. The equation is balanced.

Types of Chemical Reactions

  • Synthesis (Combination): A + B → AB
  • Decomposition: AB → A + B
  • Single Replacement: A + BC → AC + B
  • Double Replacement: AB + CD → AD + CB
  • Combustion: Fuel + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water

Examples:

Synthesis: 2Na + Cl2 → 2NaCl

Decomposition: 2H2O → 2H2 + O2

Single Replacement: Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl2 + H2

Double Replacement: AgNO3 + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO3

Combustion: CH4 + 2O2 → CO2 + 2H2O

Factors Affecting Reaction Rate

  • Temperature: Higher temperature usually increases reaction rate
  • Concentration: Higher concentration of reactants usually increases reaction rate
  • Surface Area: Greater surface area (smaller particles) increases reaction rate
  • Catalysts: Substances that increase reaction rate without being consumed

Grade 10 Chemistry Notes

Introduction to Organic Chemistry

What is Organic Chemistry?

Organic chemistry is the study of carbon-containing compounds, their properties, and reactions. Most organic compounds contain carbon and hydrogen, and many also contain oxygen, nitrogen, and other elements.

Hydrocarbons

Hydrocarbons are compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen. They are classified based on their structure:

  • Alkanes: Single bonds only (saturated)
  • Alkenes: Contain at least one carbon-carbon double bond
  • Alkynes: Contain at least one carbon-carbon triple bond
  • Aromatic: Contain benzene rings or similar structures

Alkanes

Alkanes have the general formula CnH2n+2. The first 10 alkanes are:

Name Formula Carbon Atoms
Methane CH4 1
Ethane C2H6 2
Propane C3H8 3
Butane C4H10 4
Pentane C5H12 5

Functional Groups

Functional groups are specific atoms or groups of atoms that give organic compounds their characteristic chemical properties.

  • Alcohols (-OH): Contain a hydroxyl group, e.g., ethanol (C2H5OH)
  • Carboxylic acids (-COOH): Contain a carboxyl group, e.g., acetic acid (CH3COOH)
  • Aldehydes (-CHO): Contain a formyl group, e.g., formaldehyde (HCHO)
  • Ketones (-CO-): Contain a carbonyl group between carbon atoms, e.g., acetone (CH3COCH3)
  • Esters (-COO-): Formed from alcohols and carboxylic acids, e.g., ethyl acetate (CH3COOC2H5)

Example:

Identify the functional group in C2H5OH.

The -OH group is a hydroxyl group, making this compound an alcohol (ethanol).

Isomerism

Isomers are compounds with the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements.

  • Structural isomers: Different connectivity of atoms
  • Stereoisomers: Same connectivity but different spatial arrangement

Example:

Butane (C4H10) has two structural isomers:

1. n-butane: CH3-CH2-CH2-CH3 (straight chain)

2. isobutane: CH3-CH(CH3)-CH3 (branched chain)

Chemical Solutions

Premium

What is a Solution?

A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances. The substance that dissolves is called the solute, and the substance it dissolves in is called the solvent.

Types of Solutions

  • Solid in liquid: Salt in water
  • Liquid in liquid: Alcohol in water
  • Gas in liquid: Carbon dioxide in water (carbonated drinks)
  • Gas in gas: Air (oxygen, nitrogen, etc.)
  • Solid in solid: Alloys (brass, bronze)

Concentration of Solutions

The concentration of a solution expresses the amount of solute in a given amount of solution or solvent.

Mass percent: (mass of solute ÷ mass of solution) × 100%

Molarity (M): moles of solute ÷ liters of solution

Molality (m): moles of solute ÷ kilograms of solvent

Parts per million (ppm): (mass of solute ÷ mass of solution) × 106

Example:

Calculate the molarity of a solution made by dissolving 5.85 g of NaCl in enough water to form 500 mL of solution.

Step 1: Convert mass to moles

Molar mass of NaCl = 23.0 g/mol (Na) + 35.5 g/mol (Cl) = 58.5 g/mol

Moles of NaCl = 5.85 g ÷ 58.5 g/mol = 0.1 mol

Step 2: Calculate molarity

Volume of solution = 500 mL = 0.5 L

Molarity = moles of solute ÷ liters of solution = 0.1 mol ÷ 0.5 L = 0.2 M

Solubility

Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature.

  • Saturated solution: Contains the maximum amount of dissolved solute
  • Unsaturated solution: Can dissolve more solute
  • Supersaturated solution: Contains more dissolved solute than a saturated solution (unstable)

Factors Affecting Solubility

  • Temperature: Most solid solutes become more soluble as temperature increases. Gases generally become less soluble as temperature increases.
  • Pressure: The solubility of gases increases with increasing pressure (Henry's Law).
  • Nature of solute and solvent: "Like dissolves like" - polar solutes dissolve in polar solvents, nonpolar solutes dissolve in nonpolar solvents.

Colligative Properties

Colligative properties depend on the number of solute particles, not their identity.

  • Vapor pressure lowering: Adding solute decreases the vapor pressure of the solvent
  • Boiling point elevation: Solutions have higher boiling points than pure solvents
  • Freezing point depression: Solutions have lower freezing points than pure solvents
  • Osmotic pressure: Pressure required to prevent osmosis across a semipermeable membrane

Boiling point elevation: ΔTb = Kb × m

Freezing point depression: ΔTf = Kf × m

where:

ΔT = change in temperature

Kb, Kf = constants specific to the solvent

m = molality of solution

Example:

Calculate the freezing point of a solution containing 30.0 g of glucose (C6H12O6) dissolved in 500 g of water. The freezing point constant (Kf) for water is 1.86°C/m.

Step 1: Calculate the molality

Molar mass of glucose = 180.0 g/mol

Moles of glucose = 30.0 g ÷ 180.0 g/mol = 0.167 mol

Molality = moles of solute ÷ kg of solvent = 0.167 mol ÷ 0.500 kg = 0.334 m

Step 2: Calculate the freezing point depression

ΔTf = Kf × m = 1.86°C/m × 0.334 m = 0.62°C

Step 3: Calculate the freezing point

Freezing point = 0°C - 0.62°C = -0.62°C

Electrolytes and Nonelectrolytes

  • Electrolytes: Substances that dissociate into ions in solution and conduct electricity (e.g., NaCl, HCl)
  • Nonelectrolytes: Substances that do not dissociate into ions and do not conduct electricity (e.g., sugar, alcohol)

Acid-Base Solutions

  • Acids: Proton (H+) donors, pH < 7
  • Bases: Proton acceptors or hydroxide (OH-) donors, pH > 7
  • Neutralization: Acid + Base → Salt + Water

pH = -log[H+]

pOH = -log[OH-]

pH + pOH = 14 (at 25°C)

Grade 11 Chemistry Notes

Chemical Equilibrium

Introduction to Chemical Equilibrium

Chemical equilibrium is a state in which the rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal, resulting in constant concentrations of reactants and products.

For a general reaction: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

The equilibrium constant expression is:

Kc = [C]c[D]d / [A]a[B]b

where [A], [B], [C], and [D] are the molar concentrations at equilibrium.

Interpreting Equilibrium Constants

  • Kc >> 1: Reaction favors products (equilibrium lies to the right)
  • Kc << 1: Reaction favors reactants (equilibrium lies to the left)
  • Kc ≈ 1: Significant amounts of both reactants and products are present

Example:

For the reaction 2SO2(g) + O2(g) ⇌ 2SO3(g), the equilibrium concentrations are [SO2] = 0.2 M, [O2] = 0.1 M, and [SO3] = 0.4 M. Calculate Kc.

Kc = [SO3]2 / [SO2]2[O2]

Kc = (0.4)2 / (0.2)2(0.1)

Kc = 0.16 / 0.004

Kc = 40

Since Kc > 1, the reaction favors the formation of products.

Le Chatelier's Principle

Le Chatelier's principle states that when a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change, the system will shift in a direction that counteracts the change.

Effects of various changes:

  • Concentration: Adding a reactant or removing a product shifts the equilibrium to the right (towards products). Adding a product or removing a reactant shifts the equilibrium to the left (towards reactants).
  • Pressure/Volume (for gas reactions): Increasing pressure (or decreasing volume) shifts the equilibrium towards the side with fewer gas molecules. Decreasing pressure (or increasing volume) shifts the equilibrium towards the side with more gas molecules.
  • Temperature: For exothermic reactions (ΔH < 0), increasing temperature shifts the equilibrium to the left. For endothermic reactions (ΔH > 0), increasing temperature shifts the equilibrium to the right.
  • Catalysts: Do not affect the position of equilibrium but help the system reach equilibrium faster.

Example:

Consider the equilibrium: N2(g) + 3H2(g) ⇌ 2NH3(g) + heat

Predict the effect of each change:

  1. Adding more N2: Equilibrium shifts right (towards products)
  2. Removing H2: Equilibrium shifts left (towards reactants)
  3. Increasing pressure: Equilibrium shifts right (4 gas molecules → 2 gas molecules)
  4. Increasing temperature: Equilibrium shifts left (reaction is exothermic)

Equilibrium in Acid-Base Reactions

The ionization of weak acids and bases in water establishes equilibria.

For a weak acid HA: HA + H2O ⇌ H3O+ + A-

Ka = [H3O+][A-] / [HA]

For a weak base B: B + H2O ⇌ BH+ + OH-

Kb = [BH+][OH-] / [B]

The stronger the acid or base, the larger its Ka or Kb value.

Buffer Solutions

Buffer solutions resist changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. They consist of a weak acid and its conjugate base, or a weak base and its conjugate acid.

Henderson-Hasselbalch equation: pH = pKa + log([A-] / [HA])

Example:

Calculate the pH of a buffer solution containing 0.1 M acetic acid (HC2H3O2) and 0.2 M sodium acetate (NaC2H3O2). The Ka of acetic acid is 1.8 × 10-5.

pKa = -log(1.8 × 10-5) = 4.74

pH = 4.74 + log(0.2 / 0.1) = 4.74 + log(2) = 4.74 + 0.30 = 5.04
